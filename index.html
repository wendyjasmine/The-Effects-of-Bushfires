The Effects of Bushfires




Positive Impacts of Bushfires



Bushfires trigger natural process essential to the environmental ecology for seed gemination and bush life regeneration.

The fire clears it clears thick undergrowth and opens up the canopy so sunlight can reach the forest floor, encouraging the germination and regrowth of native vegetation.

The remnants of burnt trees, with hollowed out logs, can also offer attractive habitats for birds and mammals seeking shelter and nesting.

But recently, humanity has increased the severity and length of the bushfire season. So much so that it is more destructive than productive.




Negative Effects on Flora and Fauna

Endangerment

Bushfires put scarce species at risk of extinction or endangerment, especially animals that cannot fly. Even if the animals are not killed in the fire, they will still struggle for food and shelter for a few months afterwards.

Death

3 Billion animals were killed in the Australian fires during 2019-2020. Other ecosystems were also impacted indirectly, smoke and ash can pollute water sources,removing feeding and breeding areas of aquatic animals and clog the gills of fish causing them to suffocate.

Ecosystem Change

In these conditions, it becomes survival of the fittest with such scarce food and shelter after the fires. The top predators cull out those lower on the food chain and whole habitats can be destroyed which causes irreversible damage to the ecosystems in place.

Influence on other Places

Ash from bushfires can travel long distances and affect other habitats such as the glaciers in New Zealand, smoke from bushfires in Australia travelled to New Zealand, coating the glaciers in a brown layer which accelerates the melting process.

Climate Change Feedback Loop

Climate change and these mega fires we have been seeing more recently go hand in hand, the rise in temperature means less rainfall, dryer environments which means more fuel for the fires and a longer fire period.

These mega fires release too much CO2 for the bush to cope with, most fire emissions are absorbed by the regrowth of the bush, but lately these fires have been too brutal, and it is expected that the emissions will never be absorbed.

In a typical Australian year, 104 million metric tons are emitted through the burning of fossil fuels. In 2019-2020 the bushfires along with droughts emitted 186 million tons of carbon, more than doubling Australians carbon emissions for that year.

This additional emissions only exacerbates the fire conditions creating a feedback loop of mega fires that will only get worse in the future.
